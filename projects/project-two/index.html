Methods and Results

A summary of the tracking models, evaluation pipeline, key findings, and future directions developed in the thesis.

Tracking framework

Two main filtering approaches were developed and evaluated. The first was a linear Hungarian Kalman Filter (HKF). The second was a nonlinear variant, HEKF2, designed for oscillatory signal dynamics and fixed meanline assumptions.

The underlying idea was to combine recursive state estimation with assignment between predicted and observed curve positions, so that multiple traces could be tracked across complex raster images.

Evaluation strategy

The methods were tested on synthetic raster images generated in two different ways: a sum-of-sinusoids model and a statistical Fourier-based model. Performance was assessed using Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE), success rates, and qualitative visual inspection.

This synthetic pipeline made it possible to compare the estimated trajectories with known ground truth under controlled overlap and noise conditions.

Key findings

The HKF approach showed limitations when traces overlap strongly, mainly because its state-transition model was not expressive enough to preserve the correct oscillatory behavior in difficult regions.

The HEKF2 approach improved robustness by using a sine-aware transition model and constraining each trace around a fixed meanline. This reduced drifting errors and showed promising behavior in significant overlap scenarios, although some small gaps between predicted trajectories and actual pixel traces remained.

Future directions

The report identifies several next steps: post-processing to correct drift events, realignment of estimated curves to the closest smooth pixel path, and data-driven alternatives that learn transition dynamics directly from real or synthetic trajectories.

Another promising direction is to simulate the transfer function of the Galitzine seismometer in order to generate more physically faithful synthetic seismograms with accurate ground truth.
