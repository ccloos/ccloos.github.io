Project

Recursive Curve Extraction Tool for High-Amplitude Events Analysis in Historical Seismograms

Master’s thesis project on the automated digitization of historical analog seismograms, with a focus on extracting overlapping traces from Galitzine instrument recordings.

Project context

Historical analog seismograms recorded during the 20th century preserve valuable information about earthquakes, teleseisms, and environmental phenomena such as microseisms related to storms. At the Uccle seismic station, the Galitzine seismograph produced long-period records that are scientifically useful today, but difficult to analyze because they were created for visual interpretation rather than numerical processing.

Turning these archives into usable time series requires digitizing scanned raster images whose traces may overlap, drift, break, or degrade over time. Existing tools only partly address these issues, especially in fully automatic settings.

Objective

The goal of the thesis was to design a robust automated curve-tracking method able to reconstruct seismogram traces in challenging situations, particularly when several curves overlap during high-amplitude events.

Main contributions

The project introduced a recursive tracking framework based on Kalman filtering and Hungarian matching to follow multiple curves through noisy and ambiguous image regions.

It also developed a synthetic data generation pipeline to create rasterized seismogram images with ground-truth curves, enabling controlled benchmarking of the tracking algorithms.
